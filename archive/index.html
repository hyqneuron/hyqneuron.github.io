Backpropagation, intuitively explained

To put it bluntly, backpropagation is basically a finger-pointing process.

We can consider a network with a single output unit (O), a hidden layer (H), and an input layer (I). Now, let's think of O as the boss of a company who is trying to make some decision. The people in H are O's assistants, and the people in I are the unskilled workers who simply gather data.

In the forward pass, each of O's assistants are supposed to give the boss some advice. The assistants have different views on the data provided by I, so they assign different weightage to each member in I and make use of the data they provide differently.

When the advice reaches the boss, the boss trusts the advice of some of his assistants more than that of the others. So some of the assistants' advice has a greater effect on the boss's decision, and others' have less. If the boss's decision goes wrong, he would blame more on the assistants that he trusted, and less on the assistants whose advice he did not heed much (okay, this doesn't sound like how things work in the office. But truth is that the trusted assistants had a greater influence in leading the boss to a mistake, so they should be blamed first). And if his decision turned out to be pretty good, he would attribute his success more to the people he trusted and less to the people that he did not trust.This is error propagation.

Now, as for weight correction, we can think of it this way: if I am the boss, and an assistant made a very loud advice, and he turned out to be wrong, I should trust him less. On the other hand, if an assistant made a wrong advice, but indicated that he was not certain that his advice was correct by giving a small magnitude to his advice, I should not reduce my trust in this assistant by very much. It works similarly if the assistant's advice turned out to be correct. My trust in the assistant is affected by the correctness of his advice, and by the confidence with which he gave his advice.

Intuitive explanation for momentum

I'll continue with the finger-pointing explanation of backpropagation above. If you're the boss, you don't want to trust too much an assistant who happened to get things right because of luck. Instead, you want to take consistency into consideration. If an assistant performs consistently well, you'll trust him more. And if his performance is quite unstable, then maybe you don't want to trust him much.

How to tell if an assistant got lucky, or if he actually is doing some fantastic work? Add noise to your data. Because of momentum, the lucky assistants will get lost in the noise, while the truly performing assistants will stand out. How much noise? Not so much as to distort beyond recognition the pattern that your assistant is supposed to recognize, otherwise everyone would have to rely on luck.

Also, momentum would be most effective if the data vectors presented to the network in temporal proximity contain some invariant property. Think of it this way: you're looking for vehicles in images, and you have an assistant who knows a lot about tanks but not so much about sedans. If you throw random data vectors at the network, the performance of the tank expert is going to be very stable - he always gives a loud advice when he sees a tank and mutes himself when he doesn't - but the momentum is never going to grow, because he never gets to score multiple times in a row!

This is why I want to train my network with frames of a carefully produced video, not some random data vectors.

What is pattern?

From what I've seen, few people in the machine learning community have been bothered to ask this question. We have a bunch of training vectors, and the patterns lie within, isn't that right?

Yes, we know the patterns are in there, and we sometimes give them labels. But is it not interesting, that the patterns are there in the first place? How have we animals come to be able to recognize patterns? What are patterns, really?

These questions look stupid, but since I've always enjoyed asking and answering stupid questions, I'm going ahead anyway: to me, pattern is something that remains constant when all else is changing.

When you are asked to look for a pattern in a bunch of seemingly very different objects, you are expected to come up with a rule that can be applied to all those objects. This sort of "pattern hunt" is a game we all have played when we were children. The target objects may be very very different, so they're the "changes"; while the applicability of a fixed rule is always there, so it is the "pattern".

So, if pattern is constancy amid change, how can we capture it? We pay attention to what does not change. Hmm, no, not really. Here's a better way: the things that appear in our senses leave a mark in our mind, and they wear off with time. But when they have appeared frequently enough, the marks accumulate, to such an extent that they can't be wiped out, and a pattern is formed in our head.

Now, let's move back to image recognition. If we throw 512 by 512 images at a network and expect it to capture the "constancy", we'll have a problem: the same vector may never appear twice, so there is no constancy to be captured! Really? Not really. You need just a few more things, and constancy would start appearing: downsampling, smaller receptive fields, and a hierarchy of receptive fields.

Economics

Economy, Pragmatism, and Development of Altruism

  1. Positive correlation between the development of economy and increase in altruistic behavior
  2. The better off the people are, the less the marginal cost of altruistic behavior
  3. Pure altruist cannot invade a population of non-altruists; pragmatic altruist, however, can, especially when the economy has developed to such a state that the cost of altruism is less than the potential benefit. Subsequently, a population of pragmatic altruists can be invaded by pure altruists. So, in a way, pragmatic altruists pave the way for the rise of the pure altruist, and a population of pure altruists probably can't be invaded...

Incentive Accumulation - why some things don't work when they should

In most universities around the world, before a semester starts, the students usually have choose the classes they want to take in the coming semester. The same is true in my school. The problem we have here, however, is that our online class registration system sucks. As a result, the process of course registration has become a kind of physical exercise: sometimes, it takes real stamina to get just the courses you want, because of the so many trials that are needed to ensure that the specific time slots you have chosen for your classes do not clash with each other.

All that we needed was a course planner that is capable of automatically scheduling our classes in such a way that clash is guaranteed to be eliminated, and that in case of unresolvable conflict the courses to which we have given higher priority are chosen first.

Such a course planner would not involve any difficult algorithm, because the size of the search space is quite practical. Actually, I would see it as the kind of problem that smart kids in secondary schools should be perfectly capable of solving. But the interesting thing is: here in a university, nobody has done that yet!

It's not that there isn't a real need for such a programme. Every time before semester starts you would see students on social networking websites complaining about the abhorring task that is course registration. Some of them get so frustrated by the process that you will see swear words coming out of their status updates.

So, the problem isn't so hard to solve, and the need for a solution is real, but why has no one done it yet? 

I think the answer probably lies in incentive: there is a failure of incentive build-up here in my university. You see, building such a software programme isn't so hard, but it would require a lot of time. In my case, because of the way I value my time, I wouldn't be willing to write such a programme without someone paying me at least one thousand bucks. But on the other hand, if someone else were to tell me that he has a planner programme that does just what I need, I would not hesitate to pay him 10 to 15 bucks just to be able to use his planner programme.

My school, being quite a large one, has around 20,000 undergraduate students. If, on average, that one out of ten students is willing to pay for such a programme, and that the average amount they are willing to pay is one dollar, then right there we have a 2,000-dollar incentive, which should be enough to encourage the production of more than one sensible planner programme.

Now here's why no one in my school has done it: the 2,000 dollar seems to exist, but it actually does not. Even though there may be 2000 people out there who are willing to pay, on average, one dollar for this programme, nothing exists that will convert those two thousand dollar coins into a single 2,000-dollar cheque. Each of the two thousand people may have some minor incentive to use such a planner programme, but such incentive from the two thousand people does not accumulate to produce total of two thousand dollars that is needed for some people to write a serious planner programme!

Writing... you might want to blame it on the lack of entrepreneurship in my school, but this is actually a phenomenon (failed incentive accumulation) that occurs frequently at a much larger scale, and is one that deserves a more systematic solution. KickStarter.com is a good example of incentive accumulation, but can we go beyond that small-scale stuff? writing... sleeping

Philosophy

Random thoughts on qualia

Without a formal proof (I do think the following statement is provable using a contradiction resulting from self-referencing understanding/knowledge), my intuition tells me that the existence of human subjective experience is beyond any human comprehension. In other words, I believe there is no reason that we subjectively experience what we experience. Everything there is, be it in objective existence or in our subjective experience, is a miracle.

Of course the above belief is not really relevant to the discussion of qualia, I just wanted to write it down :)

To be honest, the question on the existence of qualia and its privacy did give me a lot of headache - it is, as other philosophers have put it, a hard problem indeed. In fact, I once went so far as to believe that the existence of qualia is not provable, and that all theories on qualia are simply unfalsifiable. However, I was sure, at the same time, that my subjective experience cannot be described in physical terms. One question, then, arose: how could my physical brain become so convinced that qualia exist, if qualia can't be physically detected?

The stuff I made up, in the end, look like this:

  • The existence of qualia is physically detectable
  • The other properties of qualia are not physically measurable

The above predictions have very bizarre implications, which will require further explanation...

Anyway, in the end, I think many other things must be settled first before the arguments on qualia may come to a meaningful conclusion. An important question, I think, is this: what does it mean to perceive? (Let's avoid the impossible question of "What is perception?")
